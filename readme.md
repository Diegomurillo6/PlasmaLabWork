# Plasma Lab Projects

Since last year, I have been collaborating on plasma-related research and applied engineering projects at the Plasma Laboratory of Instituto Tecnológico de Costa Rica (TEC).

To many people's surprise, this relatively small laboratory in Costa Rica hosts a wide variety of advanced equipment for plasma science, fusion research, and applied engineering.
The picture below was taken earlier this year and shows me alongside the laboratory's Tokamak and Stellarator fusion reactors. As Dr. Iván Vargas, the laboratory's director, 
likes to point out, not many people have the opportunity to see both types of fusion devices in the same place, let alone work around them.

Beyond the impressive equipment, my experience at the Plasma Laboratory has been incredibly enriching from both an engineering and scientific perspective. 
Working in a multidisciplinary research environment has allowed me to gain experience with instrumentation, data acquisition, experimental procedures, high-voltage systems, vacuum technology, and collaborative engineering projects.

## Inertial Electrostatic Confinement Glow Discharge (IECGD) Fusor-TEC

<img width="4284" height="5712" alt="Fusor" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/9f2ae1bd-8420-4080-af59-824261f9bdc4" />

This project is based on a Farnsworth Fusor, a device that uses inertial electrostatic confinement (IEC) rather than magnetic confinement to generate plasma and enable fusion-related experiments.
Unlike Tokamaks and Stellarators, which use powerful magnetic fields to confine plasma, a fusor relies on high electric fields generated by high-voltage electrodes. 

Although fusors are not intended for large-scale energy production, they are widely used for plasma research, neutron generation, education, and material or surface treatment applications.

One of my first milestones while working on this project was successfully generating my first plasma discharge:

<img width="800" height="1066" alt="1776053523982" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/f8995acd-4e2d-4bb5-bc6c-831adb46332b" />

The image quality is not ideal because the plasma formed near the vacuum chamber wallbreaker instead of concentrating around the central electrode, where it was expected to be. 
Troubleshooting issues like this has been an important part of the project, requiring analysis of operating conditions, electrode geometry, pressure levels, and power supply settings.

In parallel, I have been working on the automation of the plasma generation process. Since plasma experiments involving high-voltage systems can generate X-rays through bremsstrahlung radiation, 
operating the equipment remotely improves both safety.

The goal of this project is to allow both manual and automatic control of the 60 kV power supply from a safe distance. To achieve this, 
I have been developing a system that combines machine vision, actuators, and a Python-based user interface. The machine vision system automatically reads voltage and current values directly from the power supply 
displays, while the actuators interact with physical controls such as dials and buttons.

Future developments include integration with Langmuir probes and other plasma diagnostics to improve plasma characterization and provide more detailed information about fusor operating conditions.
Additional work may also explore machine learning approaches for plasma control and characterization, an emerging research area that remains relatively underexplored compared to more traditional plasma diagnostics and control methods.
